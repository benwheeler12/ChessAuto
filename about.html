♛ How these puzzles are made

Every puzzle here was discovered by a program and verified by a chess engine — no human composed them.

The rules

  1. You're shown a real position from a strong Lichess game, with one of your pieces removed.
  2. Place it on any legal empty square — but choose carefully: at most two squares on the whole board win. Every other square loses.
  3. After you place the piece, your opponent moves first — so a placement that just grabs material usually gets punished.
  4. Press play and two Stockfish engines battle it out. You win only if your side delivers checkmate. Draws don't count.

Where the positions come from

We stream games from the Lichess open database, keeping decisive blitz-or-slower games where both players are rated 2300+. Every second position of each game is scored with a set of fast, engine-free features: hanging pieces, mutual attacks, pins, pressure around the kings, available checks and captures, mobility imbalance, and how far passed pawns have advanced.

The most extreme positions per feature — the sharpest, most tension-loaded moments of hundreds of games — become puzzle candidates.

How a candidate becomes a puzzle

  1. Remove one of the side-to-move's pieces (queens, rooks, minors, pawns — even the king).
  2. Have Stockfish evaluate every legal square the piece could return to, with the opponent to move.
  3. Keep the position only if placement is precise: at least one square is completely winning (+3 or better after deep verification) and no more than two squares win at all.
  4. Positions that were already decided, that win everywhere, or that win nowhere are thrown away — the vast majority.

Roughly one in six candidate positions survives. Some puzzles also ship with fully pre-computed engine playouts for every legal square; for the rest, Stockfish runs live in your browser when you press play.
